Native Resources

Resources for Struggling Alaska Natives

The Issue

Indigenous Alaskans have suffered greatly at the hands of Western colonialism, facing forced relocation, cultural suppression, and loss of autonomy. These experiences have resulted in high rates of depression, anxiety, PTSD, substance abuse, and suicide. The physical impacts of colonialism include disrupted traditional ways of life and limited access to basic necessities such as food and water.

This website strives to provide information about the history of colonialism in Alaska and its ongoing impacts on indigenous communities. We also aim to connect those seeking help with resources such as mental health services, substance abuse treatment programs, and organizations dedicated to supporting indigenous communities.

Mental Health Resources

The colonization of western settlers, as well as the forced assimilation policies of the US government, have had a profound and lasting effect on the mental health of Alaska Native communities. The trauma, social and economic challenges, and loss of traditional cultural practices have contributed to high rates of depression, anxiety, post-traumatic stress disorder (PTSD), and other mental health issues among Alaska Native people. Below is a list of different resources Native's can utilize.

Link Description
ANTHC Behavioral Health Wellness Clinic

The ANTHC Behavioral Health Wellness Clinic promotes Alaska Native wellness through compassionate and easy-to-connect to behavioral health services.

The National American Indian and Alaska Native MHTTC works with organizations and treatment practitioners involved in the delivery of mental health services to American Indian and Alaska Native individuals, families, and tribal and urban Indian communities to strengthen their capacity to deliver effective evidence-based and experience-based practices. This includes the full continuum of services spanning mental illness, prevention, treatment, and recovery support.

Addiction Resources

Alcoholism is a significant problem in many Indigenous Alaskan communities, where it has devastating effects on individuals, families, and the community as a whole. Historically, colonization and forced assimilation have disrupted traditional practices and ways of life, leading to trauma, poverty, and marginalization that can contribute to substance abuse. Alcohol is often easily accessible and culturally ingrained in social events, but many people struggle with addiction and its associated health and social problems, including domestic violence, suicide, and fetal alcohol syndrome. Addressing alcoholism in Indigenous Alaskan communities requires a holistic approach that addresses the root causes of addiction and involves cultural revitalization, community empowerment, and access to healthcare and social services.

Link Description
Office of Tribal Affairs and Policy (OTAP)

OTAP provides efficient and effective delivery of resources and services to ensure that American Indians and Alaska Natives have access to prevention, treatment, and recovery support services that reflect the best of modern science and traditional cultural practices.

This service provides referrals to local treatment facilities, support groups, and community-based organizations.

Health Resources

The health care issues faced by Native Alaskans are intricately linked to the history of Western colonialism. Forced assimilation, displacement, and exploitation of resources by Western powers have disrupted Native Alaskans' traditional way of life, leading to social and economic challenges that have negatively impacted their health outcomes. Poor access to healthcare facilities, lack of healthcare resources, and inadequate healthcare infrastructure have made it challenging for Native Alaskans to receive timely and quality care. Additionally, historical trauma, cultural stigmatization, and language barriers have led to distrust between healthcare providers and Native Alaskans, further complicating the provision of healthcare services.

Cultural Resources

Alaska Natives face a range of cultural issues that stem from the historical trauma of colonization and forced assimilation. These include the loss of traditional languages, practices, and knowledge, which has eroded cultural identity and contributed to high rates of intergenerational trauma and mental health issues. Additionally, Alaska Natives face ongoing challenges related to economic marginalization, limited access to healthcare, and discrimination in areas such as education and housing. These issues are complex and interconnected, and addressing them requires a multifaceted approach that centers the voices and needs of Alaska Native communities themselves.

Displacement Resources

Alaska Natives have faced displacement for centuries due to colonization and the forced relocation policies of the US government. Many Alaska Native communities were forced to relocate from their ancestral lands to designated areas with limited resources and opportunities. This has led to the loss of cultural identity, language, and traditional practices, as well as social, economic, and health disparities. Climate change is exacerbating the issue of displacement for Alaska Natives, as melting sea ice and rising sea levels threaten the safety and stability of coastal communities. The displacement of Alaska Natives continues to be a pressing issue, highlighting the ongoing struggle for Indigenous rights and sovereignty.

Legal Resources

Native Alaskan communities may face a range of legal issues, including land rights disputes, environmental degradation, discrimination, and inadequate access to healthcare and education. Many of these issues stem from the complex legal and political history of Alaska, including the Alaska Native Claims Settlement Act of 1971, which has resulted in ongoing debates over land ownership and resource management. Native Alaskans may also face challenges in asserting their tribal sovereignty and jurisdiction, particularly in cases involving non-Native individuals or corporations. Additionally, the effects of climate change on Alaska's ecosystems and communities have led to legal battles over resource extraction and development, as well as efforts to mitigate and adapt to the impacts of a rapidly changing environment.
